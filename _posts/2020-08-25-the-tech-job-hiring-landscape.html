As a recent graduate of what some refer to as a coding bootcamp (I’m leaving comments on the Lambda School experience for another post), I have a vested interest in understanding the current state of hiring affairs. If you’ve stumbled across this blog post from the future, I would confidently assume your familiarity with COVID-19 and its impact on everything from mundane errands to the most salient political matters. With the restrictions imposed by the virus on social proximity, hiring for jobs that would normally require an in-person interview saw a dramatic change almost overnight. When I began my foray into Lambda School’s Data Science program, I was advised to set my sights away from remote employment opportunities; with the advent of COVID-19, remote work has become the norm, albeit begrudgingly. The need for employees to stay home led to some companies carrying out layoffs, while others made plans to sell their headquarters. With these impacts in mind, I sought to investigate the current state of hiring in the tech sector, resorting to Indeed for some current insights into the tech hiring status.

Remote Work

With a quick search for your respective tech job role on Indeed, you can see a number of details about the jobs you seek, including a ‘Remote’ flag on each job card when the job poster wants to explicitly mark the job with that opportunity. I used that marker to help identify jobs that could offer remote positions, although it’s entirely possible that some jobs could be temporarily remote for the time being. The great majority of postings (more than 80%) were not marked as remote in general, but we can see a marked shift in the adoption of the ‘Remote’ tag by posts as the weeks go by.

As seen in the figure above, the proportion of jobs marked as ‘Remote’ by companies posting jobs on Indeed steadily rose from below 13% to nearly 20% (the spike in the beginning of the plot could be a result of a generalization; posts any older than 30 days get marked as ‘30+ days old’, so the true rate of change is likely hidden in that discrepancy). At the time of this post, it’s unclear whether this rise will continue. For now, it's a safe assumption that a job applied to in 2020 could be remote. Depending on the role, a job may have a higher propensity to be listed as a remote opportunity. As illustrated in the visualization below, Digital Marketing Managers and UX Designers appear to enjoy more demand as remote positions, while companies hiring Systems Engineers and Administrators likely require employees to carry out more hands on tasks, as suggested by the lower proportion of remote opportunities.

Pay

Although not necessarily related to the hiring climate, I wanted to take a look at salary ranges for the most in-demand tech roles. Where applicable, I compiled salary floors and ceilings to try and find the average lows and highs for each job category. In the following bar chart, we can see a potential reason for aspiring applicants to gravitate towards the coveted Data Scientist role; with an average salary floor of around 100 thousand per year and a ceiling well in excess of 120 thousand per year, the title holds first place in the realm of salary potential. When looking at the figure below, it's may be important to note that "Software Developer" often described some form of Web Developer position. Although there are no clear reasons for this generalization, the varied role that some developers play in the tech industry could make it challenging for some many non-technical recruiters to define the job to a higher level of specificity.

Top Companies

Out of all the job postings in the data collected from Indeed, Amazon had the most frequent posts. The large number of postings by Amazon could be due to the company's size and scope, but the frequency may also be explained by jobs with technical sounding names and descriptions that aren't "tech" jobs in the traditional sense. Location is Everything?

It's difficult to guage the impact an applicant's location has on their chances of actually landing a job. If we're observing a fleeting shift to remote work, aspiring candidates may still need to look outside of their geographic circle. I collected the locations of all the pertinent job postings onto the following figure, which can be interacted with using the controls in the upper right hand corner. The circles denoting job concentration can be toggled by clicking them on the legend. You can also mouse over the circles on the map to get more information about jobs counts in that city.

The numbers above tell a story of job availability, despite the country's economy and people experiencing challenging times. Thousands of jobs are still awaiting applications from skilled tech workers, even in locations far from major cities. I encourage those seeking employment in the tech sector during this difficult period to keep looking and learning; although hiring may have slowed, opportunities still exist for driven individuals to enter the industry.
